# Product

## Register

product

## Users

MTB riders — trail, all-mountain, and enduro. Rides regularly, owns quality suspension (RockShox, Fox, Öhlins, DVO, Manitou, Marzocchi, Cane Creek). Uses their phone on the trail, often with gloves, in bright sunlight, while mid-ride. Wants correct suspension settings fast — not a deep-dive into physics.

Secondary: beginner riders who just bought their first quality fork and have no idea what number to pump it to.

## Product Purpose

RideReady is a mobile-first MTB suspension calculator. Riders enter their weight, select a fork and/or shock model, and choose their riding style to get a recommended PSI and damping click settings. A built-in sag verification flow lets riders check and correct their setup on the trail. A "Suspension Feel" tab gives symptom-based fixes for when the bike doesn't feel right.

Success looks like: rider pulls out phone before a ride, gets their numbers in under 30 seconds, heads out confident their suspension is dialled.

## Brand Personality

Capable · Approachable · Trail-native

The brand voice is like a friend who happens to be a suspension technician — knowledgeable without being condescending. It gives a clear answer, not a range of options. It feels like it belongs on a trail, not in a lab or a product catalogue.

## Anti-references

- **Gear e-commerce** (Chain Reaction, Wiggle, CRC) — product grids, promotional banners, heavy imagery, SKU-heavy layouts. RideReady is a tool, not a shop.
- **SaaS dashboard** — cream or warm-white backgrounds, sans-serif grids, hero metrics, identical card arrays. The AI-slop version of "clean".
- **Consumer fitness** (Apple Fitness, Strava's branding) — gamification rings, gradient fills, streak UIs, neon over-energy.

## Design Principles

1. **The answer is the product.** The PSI and click count are what the rider came for. Every UI decision should reduce the distance between opening the app and getting that number.
2. **Expert, not complicated.** The app knows your fork better than you do — show the answer simply, not the methodology. Confidence through clarity, not density.
3. **Trail-side first.** Bright sun, one hand, gloves. High contrast, large targets, no precision taps. If it works at the trailhead, it works everywhere.
4. **Dark is functional.** Dark-default is not aesthetic; screens in direct sunlight reflect less and tire the eye less in dark mode. The amber brand accent pops on dark surfaces.
5. **Approachable depth.** Surface a simple answer first; reveal depth (damping clicks, sag verification, feel troubleshooting) only when the rider reaches for it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Trail-side readability takes priority: high contrast, large tap targets (minimum 44×44px), legible at arm's length in sun. Formal WCAG AA compliance is a bonus where achievable, not the primary constraint.

Reduced-motion preference respected for all animations.
